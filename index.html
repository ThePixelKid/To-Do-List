Executive summary

There are substantial advantages offered by computers and other technology but environmental and health effects are evident throughout the lifecycle of the product: extraction, manufacture, use and disposal. This site will provide a summary of the concerns and practical activities that you can undertake, as well as government/community-to-promote-better-practices programs.

Main environmental issues

  • Resource extraction: Metallic (cobalt, lithium, rare earths) mining is a disaster to natural ecosystems and people.
  • Production: Intensive energy consumption and water consumption, chemicals and industrial wastes.
  • Energy consumption: Devices and data centres use the electricity and cause greenhouse gas emissions.
  • E-waste: Incremental product development generates huge amounts of products that are difficult to handle and have toxic elements.
  • Design to become obsolete: Non repairable devices become more wasteful.

Impact of technology on human health

  • Exposure to toxins: non-formal e-waste recycling may release the heavy metals and persistent organic pollutants.
  • Occupational hazards: Mining and manufacturing employees can expose themselves to hazardous environments and chemical exposures.
  • Ergonomic problems: Musculoskeletal and eye strain problems are caused by poor work station set-ups and excessive screen time.
  • Mental health: Being always connected and facing a digital overload may bring stress and decrease the well-being.

What people are capable of doing now

Reduce

  • Only useful purchases should be made; non-power-consuming models should be used (ENERGY STAR, EPEAT).
  • Reduce the number of unnecessary cloud or server resources; disable the idle VMs and services.

Reuse

  • Repair and service devices (change battery, and/or add storage) to last longer.
  • Give out working devices to the charities or the schools.

Recycle

  • Use certified recyclers (R2, e- Stewards ), or local municipal drop-off sites.
  • Recycling printer cartridges in take-back programs.

Conserve energy & ergonomics

  • Turn on power savings options and turn off or disabilities monitors at each day.
  • Adhere to the principles of ergonomics: watch on the eye level, adjust the chair height, set up the regular breaks.

Community/ Government programs

The following are some of the activities that promote sustainability:

  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): EPR is a set of laws or programs aiming at making companies fund the collection and recycling of electronics.
  • Government procurement: Purchasing policies in the government favor products which are energy efficient and those that are repairable.
  • Recycling facilities in the area: Municipal e-waste Drop and collection.
  • Refurbishment programs Nonprofits and social enterprises that recycle used equipment.
  • Campaigns and treaties: e.g. NGO pressure (e.g. greenpeace Detox) and international regulations (Basel Convention).

Disaster mitigation plans (D1.1)

  • Extinguish: Forestall buying, select long lasting and effective merchandise.
  • Reuse/ refurbish: Subsidize the repair cafes and refuse devices donation.
  • Recycle correctly: Recyclers and the return-programs are certified.
  • Energy efficiency: Increase data centres efficiency, experience renewable energy.
  • Health and safety: Fullerton PPE, recycling facilities and standards.

Ideas of implementation in schools or small organisations

  1. Green procurement policy (indicate energy efficiency, and repairability).
  2. Design IT life cycle plan: inventory, maintenance, donation and recycling.
  3. Form joint ventures with local refurbishing or charities to have reuse programs.
  4. Conduct awareness programs and ergonomics.
  5. Measure results: electronic management of waste, longevity of device, use of energy.
